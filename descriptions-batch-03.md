WALK ID: cave-hill-belfast
PERSONA: Pete
USERNAME: PeteR63
DESCRIPTION: Belfast City Council bylaws require dogs to be kept on leads throughout the park, which catches some visitors by surprise - well worth knowing before you set out. The route from Belfast Castle car park climbs through beech woodland onto the open hillside and up to McArt's Fort, an Iron Age promontory fort with a proper panorama over Belfast Lough and the city below. Scratchy doesnt get to range free here, but at 4.5 miles its a proper day out and the summit views are genuinely worth the ascent.

WALK ID: tollymore-forest
PERSONA: Deborah
USERNAME: DeborahH
DESCRIPTION: A really lovely walk through some of the most beautiful woodland in Northern Ireland - the path winds along the River Shimna and crosses several of those distinctive arched stone bridges that make this park so special. Biscuit and Marmalade can be off the lead through the open forest sections, though the signs do ask for leads near the campsite and visitor area. There's plenty of river for a drink and a splash along the route, and the ground is kind underfoot throughout - paid parking at the Bryansford Road entrance is around £5.

WALK ID: white-park-bay
PERSONA: Morag
USERNAME: morag83
DESCRIPTION: A braw wee bay on the Causeway Coast - white sand, sea views across to Scotland on a clear day, and a clifftop path with some proper exposure above the limestone cliffs. The beach is off the lead year-round apart from the western section between May and September when it's restricted (signed on site), and the car park is wee - get there early or you'll be turning back down the road. Mind there's a cafe in Ballycastle a few miles along if you need warming up after the clifftop stretch.

WALK ID: farley-mount
PERSONA: Deborah
USERNAME: DeborahH
DESCRIPTION: A lovely mix of ancient woodland and open chalk downland west of Winchester - the path from the car park climbs through West Wood before opening out onto the grassland around the pyramid folly at the summit (the boys absolutely loved sniffing around the base of it). Cattle and ponies graze in the enclosed sections so you'll want to clip on in those areas, but it's off the lead for most of the rest. Free parking at the Sparsholt entrance and all kissing gates rather than stiles, which is genuinely helpful.

WALK ID: lepe-country-park
PERSONA: Priya
USERNAME: Priya&Pretzel
DESCRIPTION: We came specifically for the dog paddling area and Pretzel was absolutely delighted - it's a proper designated swim spot right on the Solent shore, not just a bit of muddy foreshore. The coastal path is flat all the way with gorgeous views across to the Isle of Wight, and Pretzel didn't have a single complaint about the terrain. Worth knowing that the main beach section has seasonal restrictions from April to September, but the coastal path and paddling area are open year-round - parking is paid, Hampshire County Council site.

WALK ID: savernake-forest
PERSONA: Pete
USERNAME: PeteR63
DESCRIPTION: A privately owned forest managed under Crown Forestry - the only one of its kind in England - where the wide rides are permissive paths rather than public rights of way, but access for walkers and dogs is long established and uncontested on the main routes. The Grand Avenue is the centrepiece: 3.9 miles dead straight and beech-lined, one of the longest tree avenues in the country, and Scratchy made himself busy in the understorey the whole length of it. Gated enclosures contain cattle in some sections, so its leads-on there; open forest and woodland rides everywhere else. Start from Postern Hill car park off the A346 (SN8 3HP), free.

WALK ID: valley-of-rocks-lynton
PERSONA: Jamie
USERNAME: jamieok
DESCRIPTION: Ghost was on the lead the whole way and I genuinely didn't mind - the place is so dramatically weird it holds your attention regardless of what your dog is doing. Feral mountain goats wandering between enormous rock formations, the Bristol Channel dropping away below, sculpted crags above - it's a lot. Not an off-lead walk at all due to the cliff edge and the goats, but if your dog handles a lead on dramatic terrain the views along the South West Coast Path are honestly worth it.

WALK ID: quantock-hills-staple-plain
PERSONA: Morag
USERNAME: morag83
DESCRIPTION: Proper hill ground this - open heathland ridge with views across to Wales and down to Exmoor that are genuinely grand on a clear day. WALK ID: mallards-pike-forest-of-dean
PERSONA: Pete
USERNAME: PeteR63
DESCRIPTION: Forestry England maintains full open access throughout the forest here - no restrictions, no livestock, and Scratchy can range freely from the car park to the furthest trail junction without going near a lead. The lake circuit itself is only half a mile but the waymarked forest trails beyond it extend the walk to 3.5 miles through good mixed oak and conifer, and theres a designated dog dip area on the far bank of the lake that Scratchy had found before I'd even noticed it was there. Paid car park up to £5, off the Blakeney road - follow the Go Ape signs and you're there.

WALK ID: loch-morlich-glenmore
PERSONA: Morag
USERNAME: morag83
DESCRIPTION: A braw flat circuit through Scots pine with the Cairngorm plateau as your backdrop and, unusually for a Highland loch, a proper sandy beach where Midge will spend considerably longer than you'd planned. The path is well maintained and dry underfoot for most of the year - mind the April to August bird nesting season when extra care is needed near the open grassland sections. The Glenmore Lodge cafe is right by the car park, which makes the end of the walk considerably more civilised.

WALK ID: robin-hoods-bay
PERSONA: Deborah
USERNAME: DeborahH
DESCRIPTION: One of our favourites along the Yorkshire coast - the path out from the village car park follows the Cleveland Way above the cliffs to Boggle Hole, where there's usually a stream running across the beach that the boys love to drink from. You'll want to clip on for the return section through the farm fields (cattle and sheep on that stretch), but the clifftop walk and the beach at low tide are both good off-lead. Do bear in mind the village car park fills up quickly on summer weekends - we always try to arrive by nine.

WALK ID: hamsterley-forest
PERSONA: Morag
USERNAME: morag83
DESCRIPTION: A good solid forest walk in County Durham that most visitors from outside the northeast seem to miss entirely - Hamsterley has two becks running through it and the swimming spots in summer are as good as anything in the Yorkshire Dales. Dogs can range free once you're past the visitor centre, and the Orange Trail follows the upper and lower becks the whole 4.5 miles with barely any road to worry about. The visitor centre has a cafe, and there are pubs in the nearby villages if you're making a day of it.

WALK ID: gibraltar-point-lincolnshire
PERSONA: Priya
USERNAME: Priya&Pretzel
DESCRIPTION: A rainy October weekend and it turned out to be one of the better wild beach walks we've done - the dunes stretch further than you'd expect and the whole reserve has that genuinely remote feel even though it's only a couple of miles south of Skegness. Pretzel managed the sandy paths absolutely fine and the flat trails through the dunes are great for small dogs. There's a visitor centre cafe at the car park which we were very glad of, and parking is paid at the Lincolnshire Wildlife Trust site.

WALK ID: uffington-white-horse
PERSONA: Pete
USERNAME: PeteR63
DESCRIPTION: The Ridgeway runs along the top of the Berkshire Downs here - a national trail and public right of way adjacent to one of England's oldest chalk hill figures - and dogs must be kept on leads throughout due to year-round sheep grazing on all sections of the route. Dont let that put you off: chalk downland drains remarkably well and its one of the better all-weather options in the region, firm underfoot even after prolonged rain when most other local footpaths have become a quagmire. The 4-mile circuit from the NT car park (SN7 7UK, free for members) takes in the ancient earthwork banks and gives a view down the Vale that goes on for miles.

WALK ID: blaise-castle-estate
PERSONA: Jamie
USERNAME: jamieok
DESCRIPTION: A proper gorge walk tucked into the north edge of Bristol that's easy to miss if you don't know it's there - the wooded valley drops away sharply from the road and once you're in it you could be miles from anywhere. Ghost had a run in the deeper woodland sections and a swim in the stream, which is exactly what he needs after a week of pavement. Free parking on Kings Weston Road is the move - the Coombe Dingle entrance puts you closest to the river gorge trails, and the visitor centre cafe is right there on the way back out.

WALK ID: epping-forest-chingford
PERSONA: Jamie
USERNAME: jamieok
DESCRIPTION: The ancient pollarded hornbeams in the older sections of the forest are unlike anything else near London - they look genuinely strange, like a woodland from another era, and once Ghost is among them he doesn't want to leave. Get past the visitor centre and the weekend crowds thin out fairly quickly, and there's proper off-lead running in the quieter glades once you're clear of the families near the water. Goes very busy on sunny Sundays - honestly just go on a Tuesday in autumn, and stop at the King's Oak cafe near High Beach on the way round.

WALK ID: compton-bay-iow
PERSONA: Priya
USERNAME: Priya&Pretzel
DESCRIPTION: The sandstone cliffs here come in layers of red, orange and cream that are obv stunning to photograph, and the wide sandy beach below gives Pretzel plenty of space to run about. WALK ID: crawfordsburn-country-park
PERSONA: Deborah
USERNAME: DeborahH
DESCRIPTION: A compact and lovely circuit in County Down that packs in a lot for a 2.5-mile walk - a wooded glen, a waterfall (one of the tallest in Northern Ireland, which genuinely surprised me), and a stretch of sandy beach on Belfast Lough at the end for the dogs to have a swim. Biscuit and Marmalade both went straight for the water, so do bear in mind there is a fairly steep path down to the beach that older or less mobile dogs might find hard on the way back up. Free parking at the main entrance on Bridge Road in Helen's Bay, easy to find from the village.

WALK ID: golden-cap-seatown
PERSONA: Priya
USERNAME: Priya&Pretzel
DESCRIPTION: The views from the top of the highest cliff on the south coast are honestly spectacular - you can see the Jurassic Coast stretching away in both directions and the layers of colour in the cliffs below tell their own story even if you're not a geology person. We came on a clear September day and I'll be honest, the climb from Seatown is genuinely steep over a short section - Pretzel made it but we stopped four times and his little legs were done at the top. Paid car park in Seatown and there are wooden stiles on the field approach that small dogs can squeeze past, just about.
