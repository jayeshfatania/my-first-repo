WALK ID: box-hill-loop
PERSONA: Deborah
DESCRIPTION: The climb up from the Burford Bridge car park is perfectly manageable even for older dogs, and the wide chalk grassland at the top opens out beautifully once you're past the steeper section. Biscuit and Marmalade go absolutely wild up here — there's so much space and the views across the Weald on a clear day are well worth every step of the ascent. Do bear in mind the path near the viewpoint can get busy at weekends, particularly in summer. The National Trust café at the top is dog-friendly and does decent cake, which is always a bonus.

WALK ID: richmond-park
PERSONA: Jamie
DESCRIPTION: Honestly one of those walks that actually delivers on the hype — 2,500 acres inside London is genuinely hard to argue with. The deer are everywhere and you do have to clip on when you're near the herds, but there's enough open grassland between the wood plantations that Ghost can still get a proper run. Gets very busy on sunny weekends near the Pen Ponds, so either go early or push further south toward Spankers Hill Wood where it thins out. Parking's straightforward off the A316 gate.

WALK ID: leith-hill
PERSONA: Pete
DESCRIPTION: The summit and woodland below it are split between two different access regimes — access land from the tower south, and well-maintained National Trust permissive paths for the estate below, with no ambiguity about where you're permitted to walk. The Gothic tower is the highest point in south-east England and the views to the Channel on a clear day are genuinely exceptional. Underfoot conditions on the descent toward Coldharbour can become waterlogged after prolonged rain; the clay here holds moisture well into spring. Scratchy has the full run of it in the off-season — no livestock, no shooting season complications on this land.

WALK ID: frensham-common
PERSONA: Priya
DESCRIPTION: We stumbled on this one and honestly it felt like a proper discovery — a sandy beach on a heathland lake in Surrey is not something you expect to find. Pretzel loved paddling along the shallows near the Great Pond and the path around the edge of the heath is well surfaced and easy underfoot which is ideal for smaller dogs. Do keep leads on near the roped-off nesting areas in spring, the signage is clear so its easy to navigate around them. There's a small NT car park and the whole thing makes for a really lovely half-day out — deffo one for the list.

WALK ID: hindhead-common
PERSONA: Morag
DESCRIPTION: A braw bit of heathland walking — the Devil's Punch Bowl is one of those landscapes that surprises you when you see it for the first time, a proper natural amphitheatre carved into the Surrey hills. WALK ID: ashridge-estate
PERSONA: Deborah
DESCRIPTION: Late April is the time to do this one — the bluebells in the beech woodland are genuinely extraordinary, carpeting the ground in every direction as far as you can see. The trails are well marked and the terrain is gentle, so it's suitable even for older dogs or those recovering from something (we've brought rescue dogs here from the charity on introductory walks and they've all done beautifully). Do bear in mind the car park off the B4506 fills up quickly on spring weekends, so an earlier start is sensible. The boys love snuffling through the leaf litter along the Monument Trail, which is always a bonus.

WALK ID: hampstead-heath
PERSONA: Jamie
DESCRIPTION: Parliament Hill on a winter morning when the mist is sitting over the city is genuinely one of the best things about living near London — there's nothing else quite like it. WALK ID: wimbledon-common
PERSONA: Priya
DESCRIPTION: A proper midweek reset — Pretzel gets a full run on the heather and we're back in under an hour if we do the windmill loop. WALK ID: newlands-corner
PERSONA: Pete
DESCRIPTION: Several well-waymarked rights of way head south from here across Albury Downs and toward the Silent Pool — public footpaths and bridleways, not merely permissive access. The chalk grassland above the North Downs scarp is excellent open walking and the views across the Tillingbourne Valley are as good as anything in Surrey. Stiles are present on some of the field paths toward Albury; manageable but worth knowing about. Dont attempt the lower paths through the valley floor after heavy rain as the going becomes heavy underfoot near the water meadows.

WALK ID: ranmore-common
PERSONA: Deborah
DESCRIPTION: A really satisfying woodland ridge walk that feels far more remote than it has any right to given how close it is to Dorking. The path along Ranmore Common runs above the Mole Valley with glimpses of the Denbies vineyard slopes below, and the trails are well maintained by the National Trust for much of the route. The terrain is muddy through winter — good boots are essential, and I wouldn't bring an older dog in January without expecting them to get thoroughly mucky. Full off-lead access throughout is a genuine bonus for those of us with dogs that need space to roam.

WALK ID: haytor-dartmoor
PERSONA: Morag
DESCRIPTION: Open moorland walking at its best — Haytor and Hound Tor on the same circuit gives you two dramatic granite outcrops and proper Dartmoor atmosphere. The weather changes fast up here, so your best bet is to check the forecast and not start if it's coming in from the west. Midge can be off-lead for the full route on the open moor; watch for ponies and sheep in the lower ground though, both are present. The granite tramway between the tors is an interesting bit of history underfoot if you know to look for it. Worth every step on a clear day.

WALK ID: burley-new-forest
PERSONA: Deborah
DESCRIPTION: Start from the village car park in Burley and head south into the open forest, where the ancient beech and oak quickly swallow the sound of the road. The New Forest ponies and cattle roam freely throughout and you will encounter them, so leads on when animals are nearby — this is not the place to find out whether your dog is reliable with livestock. The heathland sections beyond the forest edge are completely open and the boys absolutely love it there, but do bear in mind that ground-nesting birds are present in spring and summer. The village has a couple of dog-friendly cafés for afterwards — worth planning the walk around if you're coming for the day.

WALK ID: stanage-edge
PERSONA: Pete
DESCRIPTION: The entire ridge from Stanage End to High Neb is open access land under the CRoW Act — no footpath restrictions, full ranging rights for dogs throughout. The gritstone edge itself is one of the finest walking ridges in England, with the Hope Valley opening up to the south in a view that repays the climb every single time. Underfoot conditions on the ridge are firm and good; the moorland on the eastern slope toward Moscar can be wet underfoot after rain. Grouse shooting takes place on the moor east of the edge from 12th August; no formal closure of access land but be aware of shooting parties and keep dogs under close control during that period.

WALK ID: malham-cove
PERSONA: Morag
DESCRIPTION: The limestone pavement at the top of the cove is one of those genuinely strange landscapes — nothing quite prepares you for it the first time, these flat sheets of weathered rock criss-crossed with deep grykes. The ascent up the steps alongside the cove face is steep but short, and the path opens onto the pavement almost immediately. Gordale Scar is worth the detour — scramble through the narrow gorge and you're into quiet fell country above. Livestock present throughout the lower dale so leads on; off-lead territory begins properly once you're up on the open fell above. Not a walk for soft-pawed dogs — the limestone is hard underfoot.

WALK ID: bookham-common
PERSONA: Pete
DESCRIPTION: A Site of Special Scientific Interest managed by the National Trust — the veteran oak woodland and wet carr here are genuine ecological rarities this close to London. The ancient common land designation means unrestricted access throughout; no permissive-path complications. The boardwalk through the wetland section is well maintained and keeps you out of the bog even in winter. Underfoot conditions elsewhere on the common can be muddy from October through to March — the clay soil retains water across much of the western section. Scratchy is off-lead the whole way; no livestock on the common itself.

PERSONA: Priya

WALK ID: the-hurtwood
PERSONA: Pete
DESCRIPTION: Private land rather than CRoW access land, but the permissive access agreement here has been in place for decades and the arrangement is reliable — walkers and riders have had the run of it long enough that it functions as open access in practice. This is the largest area of open access heathland in south-east England and the ridge walk gives excellent views south toward the Weald. No livestock, no shooting complications. The heather and bilberry are at their finest in late August when the ridge goes purple. Several unmarked paths cross the ridge; the main waymarked route along the top is easy to follow. Scratchy gets a completely uninterrupted run the full length of the ridge.

WALK ID: epsom-common
PERSONA: Jamie
DESCRIPTION: This one's properly undervalued for how close it sits to central London — ancient common land with the medieval stew ponds at the centre and woodpeckers knocking about in the oaks overhead. Ghost has full off-lead run of it, there's no livestock, and on a midweek morning you can go a full hour without seeing another person. WALK ID: shere-village
PERSONA: Deborah
DESCRIPTION: A small ford with a stream running through the village centre sets the tone nicely before you climb into the National Trust woodland of Abinger Roughs above. The trails through the Roughs are muddy from autumn through spring so boots are essential, but the woodland itself is beautiful and the dogs can be off-lead through most of it. Do bear in mind there are stiles on some of the paths — nothing difficult, but worth knowing if you have a larger or less mobile dog. The Tillingbourne stream at the bottom is a favourite cooling-off spot in summer — Biscuit and Marmalade make a beeline for it every time.

WALK ID: cuckmere-haven
PERSONA: Deborah
DESCRIPTION: One of my most-recommended walks for nervous or older dogs — it's completely flat along the Cuckmere River through the nature reserve, with the chalk cliffs framing the view toward the sea at the end. The path is well-surfaced and the distance is very manageable, ideal for dogs that can't take big hills or rough ground. Do bear in mind there is livestock in some of the riverside meadows so leads on through those sections. The Seven Sisters Country Park has a café and toilets at the start — everything you need for a comfortable full day out.

WALK ID: cissbury-ring
PERSONA: Pete
DESCRIPTION: An Iron Age hillfort on open access land — the broad earth ramparts are a public right of way that circuits the entire perimeter. The interior of the fort is access land and dogs can range freely across the whole area without restriction; no livestock present in the fort itself. The views south to the sea and north across the Weald are excellent. Underfoot conditions are good year-round on the chalk and flint; firm even after wet weather compared with the clay of the valley below. The car park off Storrington Rise is free. A short but satisfying walk — Scratchy can cover the full circuit in under an hour at a good pace.
