# Walk Descriptions — New Walks (Batch 01)

20 walks. Persona assignments: Deborah ×4, Jamie ×4, Morag ×4, Pete ×4, Priya ×4.

---

WALK ID: arthurs-seat-holyrood
PERSONA: Jamie
DESCRIPTION: A 251-metre extinct volcano sitting in the middle of a capital city — the fact that this exists and you can just walk up it is still slightly unreal. The climb from Queen's Drive up the rocky path to the summit is genuinely steep in places but Ghost manages it no problem and the view from the top over the whole city and out to the Firth is worth every bit of it. Keep leads on near the two lochs at the bottom (Dunsapie especially) because of the wildfowl, but the open hillside above that is fully off-lead. Parking on Queen's Drive is free which, for an Edinburgh attraction, is basically miraculous.

---

WALK ID: threipmuir-pentland-hills
PERSONA: Deborah
DESCRIPTION: A really lovely reservoir circuit in the Pentland Hills — the Threipmuir and Harlaw loop is perfectly manageable and the scenery is far grander than the "easy" rating might suggest. Do bear in mind that sheep are present in the fields surrounding the reservoirs, particularly during lambing season in spring, so leads on when near the boundaries is the sensible approach. The boys love the open hillside above the reservoir where you can let them range properly. The car park at Mansfield Road in Balerno is free and straightforward to find.

---

WALK ID: dunkeld-hermitage
PERSONA: Morag
DESCRIPTION: Ossian's Hall perched above Black Linn Falls is one of those spots you remember — a wee folly on a rocky promontory with the River Braan thundering into the gorge below. The National Trust for Scotland woodland is full off-lead territory throughout and the giant Douglas firs make it feel properly dramatic. Underfoot it's well maintained on the main path, though some of the side routes can be slippy after rain — your feet will know about it. There's a good café back at the car park. Worth the small parking charge.

---

WALK ID: tentsmuir-forest-fife
PERSONA: Morag
DESCRIPTION: Pine forest straight onto a wide sandy beach — Tentsmuir does that combination better than almost anywhere on the east coast of Scotland. Midge gets off-lead in the forest sections and along the beach, though the NNR section near the north end of the beach requires leads to protect the grey seals and nesting birds. Your best bet is to go at low tide when the sand is firm and wide. Mind the car park charges at Kinshaldy — £4 for the day, which is fair enough given how good it is. Quiet on weekdays even in summer.

---

WALK ID: balmaha-loch-lomond
PERSONA: Morag
DESCRIPTION: Balmaha sits right on the West Highland Way and the lochshore walk south from the village gives you proper Loch Lomond views without the effort of climbing Ben Lomond. The Millennium Forest loop behind the village adds a good bit of woodland variation. Highland cattle are in the fields seasonally so leads near those sections; off-lead on the forest road and along the lochshore itself. Grand on a clear day, dreich but still worthwhile in the rain. The Oak Tree Inn in the village does food and is dog-friendly.

---

WALK ID: pen-y-fan-brecon
PERSONA: Pete
DESCRIPTION: The standard ascent from Pont ar Daf is a clear public right of way the whole way to the ridge — well waymarked, heavily used, maintained by the National Trust. Pen y Fan and Corn Du are the highest points in the Brecon Beacons and the views north over the Neuadd Reservoirs and south across the Vale of Neath are exceptional in clear conditions. Dogs must be on leads throughout this route due to sheep — there is no off-lead opportunity on the standard circuit and that should be stated plainly. The ascent gains around 450m; underfoot conditions on the main path are generally good but can be eroded near the summit. Paid NT car park at Pont ar Daf.

---

WALK ID: llyn-padarn-llanberis
PERSONA: Pete
DESCRIPTION: Padarn Country Park is managed by Gwynedd Council and the waymarked circuit through it is mostly permissive paths — well-maintained, clearly signed, and largely good underfoot year-round. The route passes Dolbadarn Castle, a genuine 13th-century Welsh fortress on a rocky bluff above the lake, which is worth a stop. Off-lead access through most of the park; leads required near nesting birds in the woodland section on the north shore. Underfoot it's good for most of the year — firm gravel and tarmac paths with some rougher ground near the quarry workings. The lake provides excellent swimming access for dogs throughout. Paid car park at the National Slate Museum.

WALK ID: rhossili-gower
PERSONA: Priya
DESCRIPTION: Three miles of wide sandy beach backed by massive green headland, with a dragon-shaped tidal island sticking out into the sea at one end — Pretzel just loved bombing around on the sand here. The beach is off-lead year-round with no seasonal restrictions which is genuinely rare for a beach this beautiful. The clifftop path from the village is well surfaced and gives you the iconic view down the full length of the bay. We got there early to beat the crowds and the light was absolutely stunning. NT car park in the village — free for members.

WALK ID: alnmouth-northumberland
PERSONA: Jamie
DESCRIPTION: One of those quietly brilliant small places that hasn't been ruined yet — a small estuary village at the mouth of the River Aln with a genuinely beautiful beach and almost no one on it outside summer. Ghost can run free the whole way and the circuit combining the beach with the riverside return path along the Aln is a really satisfying shape to a walk. Worth knowing that the tidal currents make swimming a bit sketchy, so keep an eye if your dog is a strong swimmer and keen on it. The car park on Marine Road is about £3.50 all day which is reasonable.

---

WALK ID: hardcastle-crags-hebden
PERSONA: Jamie
DESCRIPTION: Deep wooded valley, a Victorian mill at the bottom, and honestly better than it sounds on paper. This is National Trust walking in the north done right — Gibson Mill is a proper focal point and the woodland along Hebden Water is lowkey beautiful in all seasons. Leads near the café and mill area, off-lead in the woodland sections which is most of it. Gets muddy — this is West Yorkshire and the valley floor holds water, don't pretend otherwise. The NT car park at Midgehole is a bit steep at £8 for the day but Hebden Bridge right nearby has decent food if you fancy making a full day of it.

---

WALK ID: formby-beach-pinewoods
PERSONA: Deborah
DESCRIPTION: A walk that gives you two very different environments — the ancient pinewood first, then the wide open beach beyond the dunes. The pine section is a red squirrel conservation area, so dogs need to be under close control through there and ideally on leads to give the squirrels every chance, which is entirely reasonable. Once you're through to the beach, off-lead running is permitted and the space is wonderful. Do bear in mind the dunes here are some of the most impressive in England and the scramble over them is perfectly manageable for most dogs. National Trust car park at Lifeboat Road.

---

WALK ID: clumber-park-nottinghamshire
PERSONA: Deborah
DESCRIPTION: Six miles around a large estate lake sounds like alot, but the terrain is so gentle and the National Trust paths so well maintained that it never feels it — we've taken two older rescue dogs here and they completed the full circuit without any trouble. The lakeside path is particularly lovely, with the Gothic revival chapel appearing through the trees about halfway round as a proper landmark moment. There's a dog-friendly café called Central Bark on site, which the boys were very excited about. Leads near the kitchen garden area only; off-lead for the vast majority of the route.

---

WALK ID: sherwood-forest-major-oak
PERSONA: Pete
DESCRIPTION: A thousand-year-old pedunculate oak with a girth of nearly 10 metres doesn't need much introduction, but the walk through Sherwood Forest to reach it passes through genuinely impressive ancient woodland managed by Forestry England. Dogs should be on leads near the visitor centre and the Major Oak itself per RSPB management guidance, but may be off-lead in the broader Budby South Forest sections away from the main paths. The RSPB-managed car park is paid; dont let the modest entrance facilities fool you about the quality of the woodland beyond. A short walk but a significant one.

---

WALK ID: cannock-chase-birches-valley
PERSONA: Pete
DESCRIPTION: The best access point into an Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty — a vast network of waymarked trails through pine forest and open heathland, all of it open access land under CRoW. This is open access land under CRoW throughout the forest sections, with dedicated dog exercise areas in addition. No livestock anywhere on the Chase, no shooting complications. Underfoot conditions vary by trail — the forest roads are firm year-round, the heathland paths can be soft after rain. Scratchy has full off-lead access the entire route. Forestry England pay-and-display car park at Birches Valley; the Forest Café on site is dog-friendly.

---

WALK ID: lickey-hills-birmingham
PERSONA: Priya
DESCRIPTION: We didn't expect much from a country park ten minutes from Birmingham and came away genuinely impressed — the views from Beacon Hill across the city and toward the Malvern Hills are much better than you'd expect this close to an urban edge. The paths wind through mixed woodland and Pretzel managed the hills fine, though some of the steeper sections require a bit of effort for short-legged dogs so take it at their pace. Facilities are good, there's a visitor centre with a café, and the free car park means its a very easy day out without spending much. Deffo underrated.

---

WALK ID: kielder-water-forest
PERSONA: Morag
DESCRIPTION: Kielder is properly remote — the Duke's Trail heads into the largest working forest in England from the castle and you can go an entire morning without crossing another person. Midge is off-lead the whole way with no livestock to worry about, which makes a pleasant change. Underfoot it's muddy in places after rain, mind — the forestry tracks hold water and you'll need decent boots from October onwards. The forest is at its best in autumn when the larch plantations turn. Free parking at Kielder Castle visitor centre, and the café there is warm and welcome after a winter circuit.

---

WALK ID: dunwich-heath-suffolk
PERSONA: Priya
DESCRIPTION: Lowland heathland right on the Suffolk coast — the sea is visible through gaps in the scrub and the whole thing is quiet and a bit otherworldly in a way that's hard to describe but easy to feel. Pretzel loved it and the National Trust's waymarked Woof Walk means its really easy to follow without any navigation stress. Keep leads on between March and August when ground-nesting birds are present on the heath, but off-lead on the beach and outside the nesting season on the heath itself. The NT tearoom in the coastguard cottages is dog-friendly and the scones are excellent.

---

WALK ID: thetford-forest-high-lodge
PERSONA: Deborah
DESCRIPTION: Four waymarked trails from a single well-organised base, with clear signage throughout and a dedicated off-lead exercise area near the start for dogs that need a run before settling into the main walk. The Firecrest Trail at four miles gives you the best of the forest with good firm paths throughout, ideal for dogs of all ages and fitness levels. Do bear in mind dogs are on leads on the main waymarked trails outside the exercise area, so if you have an off-lead-only dog it's worth checking the layout before you go. The Forestry England café is dog-friendly.

---

WALK ID: baggy-point-croyde
PERSONA: Jamie
DESCRIPTION: A National Trust headland separating two of north Devon's best beaches — the cliff walk around the point gives you views of both, Croyde to the north and Morte Bay opening out to the south. Genuinely good walking and Ghost appreciated the wide clifftop grassland. Worth knowing that Croyde beach has a seasonal dog ban May to September so this walk is better done in shoulder season when you can access the beach section as well. There are stiles in the field sections and a bit of livestock so clip on through those bits. NT car park in Croyde village — paid, but not outrageous.

---

WALK ID: bute-park-cardiff
PERSONA: Priya
DESCRIPTION: Right next to Cardiff Castle and you genuinely forget you're in the middle of a capital city once you're into the arboretum and following the river north toward Pontcanna Fields. The Taff Trail extension makes it a proper 3-mile circuit and the path is well surfaced the whole way, ideal for Pretzel's short legs. Off-lead on the open parkland sections but leads near the café areas and road crossings — very clearly signed so no confusion about where. No on-site parking so we came in by train which took about ten minutes from Cardiff Central.
